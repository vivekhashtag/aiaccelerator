## Before You Begin: The Big Picture

Every time you run a neural network — whether it's GPT-4 generating text, Stable Diffusion drawing an image, or a ResNet-50 classifying a photo — the computation ultimately reduces to one thing happening billions of times per second inside a chip:

```
result = result + (input × weight)
```

This is the **Multiply-Accumulate (MAC) operation**. Everything else in this course — quantization, inference engines, FPGA accelerators, agentic systems — is about doing this operation faster, cheaper, and more efficiently.

This module answers the question: **at the most fundamental physical level, how does a computer actually do this?**

The answer starts with a silicon atom.

---

## What Is a Semiconductor?

Materials in our universe fall into three broad categories based on how well they conduct electricity:

**Conductors** (copper, gold, aluminium): electrons flow freely. Resistance is low.

**Insulators** (rubber, glass, pure silicon dioxide): electrons are tightly bound. Almost no current flows.

**Semiconductors** (silicon, germanium, gallium arsenide): their conductivity sits between conductors and insulators — and crucially, their conductivity can be *controlled* by adding tiny amounts of impurities or applying electric fields.

This controllability is everything. It is what makes transistors possible, and transistors are what make computers possible.

### Why Silicon?

Silicon (Si) is element 14. Its outer shell has exactly 4 electrons — it wants 4 more to be stable. In a crystal of pure silicon, each atom shares its 4 outer electrons with 4 neighbouring atoms, forming a rigid **crystal lattice**. At absolute zero, all electrons are locked in bonds — pure silicon is a perfect insulator.

### Doping: Engineering Conductivity

When we intentionally add specific impurities — a process called **doping** — we can dramatically change silicon's conductivity.

**N-type silicon:** Add phosphorus (element 15, 5 outer electrons). Four electrons bond with neighbouring silicon atoms; the fifth is loosely held and easily freed. Result: extra free electrons available to carry current. Free electrons are the majority charge carriers.

**P-type silicon:** Add boron (element 5, 3 outer electrons). Boron creates a *hole* — a position in the lattice that "wants" an electron. A nearby electron can hop into this hole, creating a new hole where it came from. Holes are the majority charge carriers.

## MOSFET: The Transistor That Runs the World

Modern digital chips use **MOSFETs** (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistors). Every processor, GPU, and AI chip is built from billions of them.

The MOSFET has three terminals:

- **Gate (G):** The control terminal. Voltage here controls whether current flows.
- **Source (S):** Where carriers originate.
- **Drain (D):** Where carriers go.

Between the gate metal and the semiconductor channel is a thin layer of silicon dioxide (SiO₂) — the oxide insulator. The gate is **electrically isolated** from the channel; it controls current through an electric field (hence "field-effect").

### N-channel MOSFET (NMOS)

**With gate voltage = 0:** The P-type channel between the two N-type regions forms two back-to-back PN junctions. No current flows. The MOSFET is **OFF**.

**With gate voltage ≥ threshold (Vth, typically 0.3–0.7 V):** The positive gate voltage attracts electrons to the surface of the P-type channel, forming a thin layer of N-type material — the **inversion layer**. Now there is a continuous N-type path from source to drain. Current flows. The MOSFET is **ON**.

In digital circuits: NMOS is ON when gate is HIGH (logic 1), OFF when gate is LOW (logic 0).
