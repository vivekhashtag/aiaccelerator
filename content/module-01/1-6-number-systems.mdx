## Everything Is Bits

A digital wire carries one of two voltages: HIGH (1) or LOW (0). Every integer, fraction, character, image, and neural-network weight must ultimately become a sequence of these **bits**. How we encode numbers in bits is not academic trivia — for AI it directly sets the **model's memory footprint, compute throughput, latency, and accuracy.**

---

## Binary and Hexadecimal

Each bit position is a power of two. An 8-bit number's place values:

<BinaryPlaceValue caption="Each lit bit adds its place value. 10110101 = 128 + 32 + 16 + 4 + 1 = 181 — and grouped into nibbles, that's 0xB5 in hex." />

**Hexadecimal (base 16)** is shorthand: each hex digit is exactly 4 bits (a nibble), so `0xB5 = 1011 0101 = 181`. GPU memory addresses, weight-file formats, and hardware registers are almost always shown in hex.

---

## Representing Negative Numbers

Three schemes, each better than the last:

- **Sign-magnitude** — top bit is the sign, rest is magnitude. Problem: *two* zeros (+0 and −0), and the adder must special-case the sign. Essentially unused today.
- **One's complement** — negate by inverting every bit. Still two zeros, and addition needs an awkward "end-around carry."
- **Two's complement** — negate by inverting every bit **and adding 1**. This is what every modern computer uses.

<TwosComplementDiagram caption="To negate in two's complement: invert every bit, then add 1. The result for −5 is 11111011 — and adding it to +5 wraps cleanly back to zero." />

<Callout type="insight" title="Why two's complement won">

The addition circuit is **identical** for signed and unsigned numbers — the same adder you built in Topic 1.3 just works, no sign handling needed. There is exactly **one** zero. And an N-bit value cleanly spans −2^(N−1) to +2^(N−1)−1 (for 8 bits: −128 to +127). To widen a number, **sign-extend** by replicating the top bit. Simplicity in hardware is why it's universal.

</Callout>

## Fixed-Point Numbers

A **fixed-point** number puts the binary point at a *fixed* position. **Q(m).(n)** notation means m integer bits and n fractional bits.

For Q8.8 (16 bits): `0000 0001 . 1000 0000` → integer part `1`, fraction part `0.5` → value **1.5**.

<Callout type="why" title="Fixed-point is an AI hardware shortcut">

Fixed-point arithmetic reuses the *same integer adders and multipliers* — no dedicated floating-point unit needed. Integer multipliers are far smaller and lower-power than floating-point ones. If a model's weights survive in Q4.4 or Q2.6 without unacceptable accuracy loss, the hardware gets dramatically cheaper. FPGA and custom-ASIC accelerators (and FINN in Module 8) lean heavily on fixed-point with programmable precision.

</Callout>

## Floating-Point: IEEE 754

For training and high-precision inference we use **floating-point** — scientific notation in binary, with three fields:

<Formula label="IEEE 754 value" caption="Sign picks direction, exponent scales by a power of two, mantissa holds the significant digits.">value = (−1)^S × 2^(E − bias) × 1.M</Formula>

The leading `1.` is *implicit* (numbers are normalised so the top bit is always 1), buying one extra bit of precision for free.

**FP32 (single precision):** 1 sign bit, 8 exponent bits (bias 127), 23 mantissa bits → ~7 significant decimal digits, range ~10⁻³⁸ to ~10³⁸. Special encodings carve out ±0, ±∞, NaN, and denormals.

<FloatFormatsDiagram caption="Same three fields — sign, exponent, mantissa — sliced differently. BF16 and FP16 are both 16 bits, but BF16 spends them on FP32's full exponent (range) while FP16 spends them on mantissa (precision)." />

<Callout type="insight" title="BF16 — the format that made modern training practical">

**BF16 (Brain Float 16)** keeps FP32's full 8-bit exponent but truncates the mantissa to 7 bits. Genius move: converting FP32→BF16 is just *dropping the bottom 16 bits*, and because the exponent range matches FP32 there is **no overflow and no loss-scaling needed during training** — the headache that plagues FP16. Half the size means twice as many values fit in GPU memory and simpler hardware. An A100 does **312 TFLOPS in BF16 vs 77.6 in FP32**. BF16 is now the default for AI training across NVIDIA, Google TPU, and AMD.

</Callout>

FP16, by contrast, has a tiny max value of 65,504 — gradients overflow during training — so it's mostly used for *inference*, after the model is trained. The frontier (NVIDIA H100, 2022) pushes to **FP8**, doubling BF16 throughput with careful scaling.

---

## Integer Formats: Going Even Smaller

For *inference* we can drop floating-point entirely and quantize to integers:

| Format | Bits | 7B model size | Use case |
|---|---|---|---|
| FP32 | 32 | 28 GB | Training reference |
| BF16 / FP16 | 16 | 14 GB | Training / inference |
| INT8 | 8 | 7 GB | Efficient inference |
| INT4 | 4 | 3.5 GB | Edge inference |
| 1-bit | 1 | ~1 GB | BitNet (experimental) |

INT8 quantization maps floats to the range [−128, 127] with `quantized = round(value / scale) + zero_point`, where scale and zero_point come from calibrating on the value distribution (the full story is Module 6).

<Callout type="key" title="Number format is an AI engineering decision">

Precision is a direct trade. Fewer bits → smaller model, more MACs per chip (an INT8 multiplier is ~4× smaller than an FP32 one), lower latency and cost — at some risk to accuracy. Choosing FP32 vs BF16 vs INT8 vs INT4 is one of the highest-leverage decisions in deploying a model, and it's why this humble topic underpins everything in Modules 6–8.

</Callout>
