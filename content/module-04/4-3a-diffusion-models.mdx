## The Denoising Idea

GANs make sharp images but are temperamental — a moving adversarial target with no stable loss curve (4.3). **Diffusion models** reach even higher fidelity *and* train with a plain, stable regression loss. They get there with a counter-intuitive move: instead of learning to generate in one shot, they learn to **undo noise**.

The recipe has two halves:

- A **forward process** that gradually destroys a real image by adding small amounts of Gaussian noise over many steps, until nothing but noise remains. This half is **fixed** — there is no learning in it at all.
- A **reverse process** that learns to walk that chain backwards: given a noisy image, predict the noise that was added, subtract a bit of it, and repeat. Run it from pure noise and a brand-new image emerges.

<DiffusionForwardReverse caption="Forward: a fixed Markov chain corrupts the data into Gaussian noise. Reverse: a neural network learns the one thing the forward process can't be solved for analytically — how to step back toward the data. Generation is just running the learned reverse chain from random noise." />

<Callout type="insight" title="Why split a hard problem into many tiny ones">

Generating a coherent 512×512 image in a single forward pass (as a GAN does) is a brutally hard mapping. Diffusion replaces it with ~1000 **trivially easy** problems: "this image is slightly noisy — what's the noise?" Each step asks the network to remove only a whisker of corruption, so each step is learnable and the errors don't compound the way a one-shot generator's do. The price is paid at sampling time: you must run the network hundreds of times (the central cost, addressed below).

</Callout>

## The Forward Process — Fixed, No Learning

At each step the forward process adds Gaussian noise scaled by a small **variance schedule** `β_t`:

<Formula label="One forward step" caption="Each step nudges the image toward noise; βₜ grows from ~1e-4 to ~0.02 over T steps.">q(xₜ | xₜ₋₁) = 𝒩( √(1 − βₜ)·xₜ₋₁ ,  βₜ·I )</Formula>

The crucial algebraic gift: because each step is Gaussian, you can jump to **any** noise level `t` in closed form without simulating the chain. With `αₜ = 1 − βₜ` and `ᾱₜ = ∏ αₛ`:

<Formula label="Jump straight to step t" caption="One ε sample gives xₜ directly — this is what makes training cheap.">xₜ = √(ᾱₜ)·x₀ + √(1 − ᾱₜ)·ε,    ε ~ 𝒩(0, I)</Formula>

This is why training is fast: pick a random image `x₀`, pick a random timestep `t`, draw one noise sample `ε`, and you instantly have the training pair `(xₜ, ε)` — no need to roll the chain forward step by step.

## The Reverse Process — Predict the Noise

The network `ε_θ(xₜ, t)` is trained to look at a noisy image and its timestep and **predict the noise that was added**. The loss is astonishingly simple — just mean-squared error between the true and predicted noise:

<Formula label="The DDPM training objective" caption="Sample x₀, t, and ε; corrupt; ask the net to recover ε. Plain regression — no adversary, no discriminator.">L = 𝔼[ ‖ ε − ε_θ( √(ᾱₜ)·x₀ + √(1−ᾱₜ)·ε ,  t ) ‖² ]</Formula>

<Callout type="why" title="Why predict the noise instead of the clean image?">

You could train the net to output the denoised image `x₀` directly, and some formulations do. But predicting the **noise** `ε` works far better in practice: the target has a fixed unit scale at every timestep, which keeps the loss well-conditioned across the whole schedule, and it ties the objective to a clean theoretical result — minimising this MSE is equivalent (up to weighting) to learning the **score** ∇ log p(xₜ), the direction toward higher data density. Same network, a much friendlier optimisation landscape.

</Callout>

To **sample**, start from pure Gaussian noise `x_T` and iterate the learned reverse step — predict the noise, remove a calibrated fraction, add back a touch of stochasticity — from `t = T` down to `0`. The final `x₀` is your generated image.

## The Denoiser Is a U-Net

`ε_θ` is almost always a **U-Net**: a convolutional encoder that downsamples to a low-resolution bottleneck, a decoder that upsamples back, and **skip connections** that hand fine spatial detail straight across. Predicting per-pixel noise needs both global structure (from the bottleneck) and pixel-accurate detail (from the skips) — the U-Net delivers both.

<UNetDenoiser caption="The same U-Net weights are invoked at every one of the hundreds of denoising steps. The timestep t enters as a sinusoidal embedding (so the net knows how much noise to expect); text or class conditioning enters via cross-attention so the denoising can be steered." />

Two extra inputs make it controllable:

- **Timestep `t`** — encoded as a sinusoidal embedding and injected into every block, so one network can handle every noise level.
- **Conditioning** — a text prompt (encoded by CLIP), a class label, or another image — fed in through **cross-attention** so the denoiser pulls the output toward what you asked for.

<Callout type="key" title="Classifier-free guidance — the realism dial">

Modern text-to-image quality hinges on **classifier-free guidance (CFG)**. During training the model randomly drops the text condition ~10% of the time, so it learns *both* a conditioned predictor `ε_θ(xₜ, c)` and an unconditional one `ε_θ(xₜ, ∅)`. At sampling you extrapolate along the difference:

`ε̂ = ε_θ(xₜ, ∅) + w · ( ε_θ(xₜ, c) − ε_θ(xₜ, ∅) )`

The guidance scale `w` (typically 5–12) is the knob users feel as "how hard does it follow the prompt." Too low → vague, off-prompt images; too high → over-saturated, fried-looking ones. It's the single most important sampling hyperparameter.

</Callout>

## Latent Diffusion — Why Stable Diffusion Runs on Your Laptop

Running the full reverse chain in **pixel space** at 512×512 is ruinously expensive — hundreds of U-Net passes over 786,432 values. **Latent Diffusion Models (LDM)** — the architecture behind **Stable Diffusion** — fix this by doing all the diffusion in the compact latent space of a pretrained VAE (4.2):

<LatentDiffusionPipeline caption="A VAE compresses the image to a 64×64×4 latent (≈48× fewer values); diffusion runs entirely in that cheap space; the VAE decoder reconstructs pixels just once at the very end. Text conditioning enters the latent U-Net via cross-attention." />

The payoff is enormous: ~48× fewer values per denoising step turns a datacenter job into something that fits on a single consumer GPU, with almost no loss in perceptual quality. This one design choice is most of why image generation went mainstream in 2022.

<Callout type="pitfall" title="Sampling speed is diffusion's Achilles' heel">

Every denoising step is a full network call, so a 1000-step **DDPM** sampler is slow. The entire sampler-research race is "fewer steps, same quality": **DDIM** reframes sampling as a deterministic process that skips steps (~20–50), and distillation methods (progressive / consistency / latent-consistency models) push toward **1–4 step** generation. Knowing your sampler and step count is the practical lever between a 5-second and a 50-second image.

</Callout>

<DiffusionSampling caption="DDPM takes ~1000 tiny stochastic steps for top quality; DDIM takes ~20–50 larger deterministic steps for near-identical results 20–50× faster. Step count is the dial between latency and fidelity." />

## Diffusion vs GAN vs VAE

The three generative families trade off along the same axes — and diffusion wins on most of them, paying only in sampling speed:

The architecture also generalises past images. The latest systems swap the U-Net for a **transformer backbone** (a **DiT** — Diffusion Transformer), which is what scales today's frontier image and video models — directly reusing the attention machinery from the next lesson.

## Recap

- Diffusion **destroys** data with a fixed Gaussian forward process, then learns a **reverse** process to undo it one small step at a time — turning one hard generation problem into many easy denoising problems.
- The forward step `q(xₜ|xₜ₋₁)` is fixed and has a closed form `xₜ = √(ᾱₜ)x₀ + √(1−ᾱₜ)ε`, so training pairs are free to sample.
- The network `ε_θ(xₜ, t)` **predicts the noise** under a plain **MSE** loss — stable, no adversary — which is equivalent to learning the data **score**.
- The denoiser is a **U-Net** (skips for detail) conditioned on the timestep and, via **cross-attention**, on text; **classifier-free guidance** is the prompt-adherence dial.
- **Latent diffusion** runs the whole chain in a VAE's compact latent space — the trick that made **Stable Diffusion** run on consumer hardware. The remaining cost is **iterative sampling**, which DDIM and distillation attack.
