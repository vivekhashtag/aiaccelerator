## The Fundamental Tension

**Latency** is the time for one request to return; **throughput** is requests processed per second. They pull against each other, and understanding why is the conceptual foundation for everything that follows.

Maximising throughput requires **batching** — accumulating requests so the GPU processes many at once. But batching *adds* latency: the first request must wait for the others to arrive. Concretely:

<Formula label="The tradeoff, in numbers" caption="Batching raises GPU utilisation and throughput, but each request now pays the accumulation wait.">batch=1: ~15 ms latency, ~20 RPS (GPU 25% util)   ·   batch=32: ~115 ms latency, ~278 RPS (GPU 85% util)</Formula>

There's no escaping this with a clever trick — only a **frontier** of optimal configurations to choose a point on:

<LatencyThroughputPareto caption="Each point is a batch-size / timeout setting. You can trade latency for throughput along the curve, but you cannot beat it — points outside are impossible for this hardware+model, points inside are wasteful. Your job: find where your SLO lets you sit, then maximise throughput there." />

## Little's Law: The Most Important Formula in Systems Performance

Every inference engineer should have this memorised:

<Formula label="Little's Law" caption="Requests in the system = arrival rate × time each spends in it. Always true, no assumptions.">L = λ · W   (L = requests in system, λ = arrival rate, W = latency)</Formula>

At 100 RPS with 50 ms latency, there are `100 × 0.050 = 5` requests in flight on average. Simple — but its consequence for **utilisation** is profound. For an M/M/1 queue, average wait is:

<Formula label="Why latency explodes near capacity" caption="As utilisation ρ → 1, the denominator → 0 and latency diverges.">W = service_time / (1 − ρ),   ρ = utilisation = λ / μ</Formula>

<LittlesLaw caption="Latency is flat and friendly until ~70% utilisation, then turns vertical. At ρ=0.5 wait is 2× service time; at ρ=0.9 it's 10×; at ρ=0.95 it's 20×. Queuing theory guarantees the cliff — you cannot engineer it away, only stay left of it." />

<Callout type="key" title="Size your fleet for 75–80% utilisation, never higher">

The hockey-stick is not a bug to fix — it's a mathematical guarantee. With a 15 ms service time and a 200 ms P99 SLO, running at 95% utilisation gives a P99 around 1200 ms (system looks broken); at 75–80% it stays within budget. So the rule is counterintuitive but firm: **leave 20–25% of your GPUs apparently "idle."** That headroom is what absorbs traffic spikes without a P99 blowup. Engineers who pack utilisation to 95% to "save money" cause the outages that cost far more. This headroom is also why real-time inference (Lesson 5.1) sometimes sacrifices utilisation on purpose.

</Callout>

## The Three LLM Throughput Metrics (Stop Confusing Them)

LLMs have three throughput numbers, and conflating them causes bad capacity decisions:

<StatGrid items={[
  { value: "Prefill", label: "prompt tokens/sec", sub: "compute-bound ∝ TFLOPS" },
  { value: "Decode (aggregate)", label: "output tokens/sec, all users", sub: "bandwidth-bound, ~fixed" },
  { value: "Per-user output", label: "aggregate ÷ users", sub: "drops as batch grows" }
]} caption="For Llama-3-8B on A100: prefill ≈ 15,600 tok/s (compute), aggregate decode ≈ 100 tok/s (bandwidth). At batch 10 that's 10 tok/s each; at batch 100 it's 1 tok/s each — too slow." />

The critical insight: **aggregate decode throughput is fixed by memory bandwidth, regardless of batch size**. Batching amortises KV-cache overhead and improves efficiency, but it cannot raise the fundamental bandwidth ceiling. More users sharing one GPU means each gets a thinner slice of the same fixed decode budget.

## SLOs: Business Requirements as Numbers

An **SLO** (Service Level Objective) defines what "working" means. The discipline is to work *backwards* from the SLO to hardware:

## Dynamic Batching: Throughput Without the Latency Explosion

Static batching (wait for exactly N requests) stalls when traffic is light — the Nth request may never come. **Dynamic batching** runs inference when *either* the batch is full *or* a timeout expires — the production resolution.

<DynamicBatching caption="Requests accumulate in a queue; the GPU fires when batch_size is reached OR max_wait_ms elapses, whichever comes first. The timeout caps the latency penalty while still capturing throughput when traffic is heavy." />

<Formula label="The one tunable that matters" caption="max_wait_ms trades latency for throughput; tune it to your arrival rate and SLO.">max_wait_ms = 0 → batch≈1 (min latency)   ·   = 10 ms → bigger batches (more throughput)   ·   = 1000 ms → terrible for interactive</Formula>

The art is matching `max_wait_ms` to arrival rate. At 100 RPS, a 10 ms wait accumulates ~1 request — barely helps. At 1000 RPS, the same 10 ms accumulates ~10 requests — nearly a **10× throughput gain** for 10 ms of added latency. Same knob, opposite value, depending on load. This is the core of every production inference server (Lesson 5.7).

## Recap

- **Latency vs throughput** is the central tension: batching raises throughput and utilisation but adds an accumulation wait — you choose a point on a **Pareto frontier** you can't beat.
- **Little's Law** (`L = λW`) plus the utilisation equation (`W = service/(1−ρ)`) means latency **explodes near capacity** — size fleets for **75–80%** utilisation to protect P99.
- LLMs have **three** throughput metrics — prefill (compute-bound), aggregate decode (bandwidth-bound, **fixed**), and per-user output (drops with batch) — don't conflate them.
- Derive hardware from **SLOs** by working backwards; **dynamic batching** (fire on full-batch *or* timeout) captures throughput while capping latency, with `max_wait_ms` as the key knob.
