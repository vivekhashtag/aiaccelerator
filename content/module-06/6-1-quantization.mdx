## What Quantization Does

Quantization maps a continuous float to a discrete integer. It works because network weights and activations aren't spread uniformly across the float range â€” they **cluster near zero** (roughly Gaussian/Laplace), so a handful of integer levels captures almost all the useful information. Given a tensor with range `[x_min, x_max]`:

<Formula label="Affine quantization" caption="scale sets the step size; zero_point aligns float-zero to an integer. Quantize then dequantize to recover an approximate float.">scale = (x_max âˆ’ x_min) / (q_max âˆ’ q_min);  Q(x) = clip(round(x/scale) + zero_point, q_min, q_max)</Formula>

The error from this rounding is bounded by **half a step**, and that single fact explains why INT8 is nearly free while INT4 is hard:

## Granularity: the Most Important Calibration Choice

A single scale for an entire weight matrix (**per-tensor**) is wasteful: one channel with a wide range forces a coarse scale on every other channel. Using **one scale per output channel** (per-channel), or per small **group** of weights, lets each region use its own optimal step:

<QuantGranularity caption="Per-tensor: one scale for everything (coarse). Per-channel: one scale per row â€” the INT8 default, ~4Ã— lower error than per-tensor on ResNet-50. Per-group (G=32 or 128): one scale per small block â€” essential for INT4 (GPTQ/AWQ/GGUF Q4_K_M), at a tiny overhead of ~0.5 extra bits/weight." />

<Callout type="why" title="Symmetric vs asymmetric â€” and where each fits">

**Symmetric** quantization pins `zero_point = 0` (the int range is centred on zero); **asymmetric** lets zero_point float so the full integer range covers an off-centre distribution. The rule of thumb PyTorch uses: **symmetric for weights** (they're roughly zero-centred, so symmetry wastes nothing and a zero-point-free matmul is cheaper) and **asymmetric for activations** (post-ReLU activations live in `[0, x_max]`, so symmetry would throw away half the levels).

</Callout>

## PTQ vs QAT

There are two ways to get a quantized model. **Post-Training Quantization (PTQ)** quantizes a finished model â€” no retraining, seconds to minutes, just a small **calibration set** to measure activation ranges. **Quantization-Aware Training (QAT)** simulates quantization *during* fine-tuning so the model learns to be robust to rounding before it ever runs in INT8:

<PTQvsQAT caption="PTQ is the default â€” fast, no training, and usually within 0.5% for INT8. When that's not enough, QAT inserts 'fake-quantize' nodes into the training loop so weights migrate away from quantization boundaries; it recovers most of the lost accuracy at the cost of a 5â€“15 epoch fine-tune." />

<Callout type="key" title="The straight-through estimator makes QAT possible">

`round()` has zero gradient almost everywhere â€” backprop through it would kill all learning. The **straight-through estimator (STE)** simply pretends `round()` is the identity on the backward pass (`âˆ‚y/âˆ‚x â‰ˆ 1`). It's mathematically "wrong" but works: the gradient still points the right way, so the model learns to push weights into the centre of quantization bins where rounding hurts least. Use a learning rate of ~0.1â€“1% of the original â€” too high and the model forgets its FP32 knowledge.

</Callout>

**Calibration matters as much as the algorithm.** The calibration set establishes activation ranges; if it isn't representative, ranges are miscalibrated and production inputs get clipped. The method also matters: **MinMax** is simple but one outlier widens the range for everything; **percentile** clips the tail (e.g. 99.9th); **KL-divergence** (TensorRT's default) finds the clipping threshold that best preserves the distribution; **MSE** directly minimises reconstruction error. 100â€“500 samples is plenty.

## The LLM Problem: Activation Outliers

Standard INT8 *fails* on large transformers because of **activation outliers** â€” a tiny fraction of channels (~1%) consistently produce huge values (up to Â±150 while the rest sit in Â±1). A per-tensor scale forced to cover Â±150 leaves the normal channels with **under one usable INT8 level** â€” catastrophic.

<SmoothQuantShift caption="SmoothQuant rewrites Y = XÂ·W as Y = (X/s)Â·(WÂ·s) â€” mathematically identical, but the per-channel factor s pulls the activation outliers down and pushes the difficulty into the weights, which are easy to quantize offline. On LLaMA-2-13B it takes naive-INT8's 41% MMLU back to 54.3%, within 0.5% of FP16." />

For **INT4** LLM weights, two methods dominate:

<Callout type="key" title="GPTQ vs AWQ â€” INT4 that actually works">

**GPTQ** quantizes weights column-by-column and, after each column, **compensates** by nudging the remaining unquantized weights to absorb the error (using the layer's Hessian from calibration data). Accurate, but slow (Cholesky + column sweeps) and memory-heavy (stores the Hessian). **AWQ** instead *prevents* error: it identifies the ~1% of **high-salience weights** (those multiplied by large activations) and scales them up to get more INT4 resolution, scaling the matching activations down to keep the output unchanged. AWQ is far faster (just per-channel scaling), has a fused dequant+matmul kernel, and edges GPTQ on downstream tasks. **Practical default for 2025: AWQ â€” Q4_K_M (GGUF) for CPU/edge, AWQ + vLLM for GPU.**

</Callout>

## Recap

- **Quantization is the highest-impact inference optimization** â€” INT8 typically costs &lt;0.5% accuracy for 2â€“4Ã— speed and memory; reach for it first.
- It maps floats to integers with `Q(x) = round(x/scale) + zero_point`; rounding error is **â‰ˆ scale/2**, which is why INT8 (256 levels) is nearly free but INT4 (16 levels) is 17Ã— coarser and needs smarter methods.
- **Granularity** is the key calibration knob: per-channel (INT8 default) and per-group (INT4 essential) beat per-tensor by giving each region its own scale; use symmetric for weights, asymmetric for activations.
- **PTQ** is fast and usually enough; **QAT** (via the **STE**) recovers accuracy when PTQ falls short. Calibration data and method (KL/MSE/percentile) matter.
- LLMs need extra care: **SmoothQuant** migrates activation outliers into the weights for viable INT8; **GPTQ** (error compensation) and **AWQ** (salience-aware scaling) make INT4 serving practical.
