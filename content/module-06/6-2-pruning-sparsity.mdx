## Two Axes of Compression

Quantization (6.1) shrinks each weight; **pruning** removes weights entirely — zeroing the parameters that contribute least to the output. The lever is intuitive (a 50% sparse model has half the weights), but the payoff depends entirely on *which* weights you remove and whether your hardware can skip them. That subtlety is the whole lesson.

---

## Unstructured Pruning — and Why It Disappoints

The simplest method is **magnitude pruning**: zero out the smallest-absolute-value weights, anywhere they appear. It compresses well and barely dents accuracy — networks are heavily **overparameterised**.

<Callout type="insight" title="The lottery ticket hypothesis">

Frankle & Carbin (2019) showed that inside a large trained network hides a sparse **"winning ticket"** subnetwork that, retrained *from its original initialisation*, matches the full network's accuracy. Found by iterative magnitude pruning (train → prune → reset survivors to init → retrain), it's strong evidence that most of a network's capacity is redundant — the justification for pruning at all.

</Callout>

But there's a catch that trips up most newcomers:

<UnstructuredVsStructured caption="Scattered zeros (left) don't help on dense hardware: cuBLAS and Tensor Cores operate on dense rectangular blocks, so a randomly-sparse matrix occupies the same VRAM and runs the same multiply-add cycles — 0 × x = 0 is still computed and accumulated. Removing whole units (right) yields a genuinely smaller dense matrix that's faster on any hardware." />

<Callout type="pitfall" title="50% unstructured sparsity ≠ 2× faster">

This is the single most common pruning mistake. A weight set to zero in a random position still gets loaded and multiplied — the GPU has no efficient way to skip arbitrary zeros. Unstructured pruning helps **model size on disk** (zeros compress well) but, on its own, gives **no inference speedup** on GPUs. To get speed you need *structure*.

</Callout>

## The Hardware Exception: NVIDIA 2:4

Ampere (A100) and newer GPUs accelerate exactly one fine-grained pattern: **2:4 structured sparsity** — within every contiguous block of 4 weights, exactly 2 are zero. A small index tells the sparse Tensor Core which 2 to keep:

<Sparsity24 caption="2:4 sparsity does 2 MACs instead of 4 per group plus a tiny index — a real 2× throughput at exactly 50% sparsity. It must be imposed during training (the network learns which 2-of-4 to keep), not bolted on afterward. The A100's sparse INT8 path hits 1,248 TOPS vs 624 dense." />

## Structured Pruning — Real Speedups

To get speedups on *ordinary* hardware, remove whole structural units so the result is a smaller **dense** model:

<StructuredPruningUnits caption="Channel pruning drops the conv filters with the smallest L1 norm; head pruning removes the attention heads doing the least selective work (low gradient×activation, or high attention entropy); layer dropping removes whole transformer layers (early/late ones contribute least). Each shrinks the model into a smaller dense one." />

<Callout type="why" title="Which heads, which channels?">

For **channels**, the L1 norm of each filter's weights is a cheap, effective importance score. For **attention heads**, three signals identify deadweight: *gradient × activation* (first-order contribution to the loss), *attention entropy* (a near-uniform head isn't selecting anything), and *Taylor-expansion proxy* (the head whose removal least changes the loss). For **layers**, the middle layers of a deep transformer matter most, so dropping alternate early/late layers is the aggressive option — exactly what DistilBERT does (next lesson).

</Callout>

## Gradual Magnitude Pruning

In practice the most effective approach isn't a single post-hoc cut — it's **gradual magnitude pruning (GMP)**: ramp the sparsity up *during* training so the model continually adapts to its shrinking weight set:

<GradualPruningSchedule caption="Sparsity rises on a cubic schedule (slow start, fast middle, slow finish) — e.g. 0% → 70% over 50 epochs. After each prune step the model retrains and recovers, so the final sparse network is far healthier than one pruned all at once." />

## Sparsity × Quantization = Multiplicative

Pruning and quantization attack different things (count vs precision), so they **stack**:

<Callout type="key" title="The honest summary">

**Structured pruning** (channel/head/layer) gives real, portable speedups at a 0.5–2% accuracy cost and is the version that matters for most deployments. **Unstructured pruning** only pays off with NVIDIA's 2:4 hardware support — otherwise it's a model-size win, not a latency win. And **gradual** beats post-hoc every time. Always pair pruning with the benchmarking protocol from Module 5: measure the *actual* speedup, not the theoretical one.

</Callout>

## Recap

- **Pruning removes parameters; quantization reduces their precision** — complementary axes that multiply.
- **Unstructured** magnitude pruning compresses well (lottery-ticket overparameterisation) but gives **no GPU speedup** — dense kernels still multiply the scattered zeros.
- The exception is **NVIDIA 2:4** structured sparsity (2 of every 4 weights), a real 2× on Ampere+ that must be **trained in**.
- **Structured** pruning of whole **channels, heads, or layers** yields a smaller *dense* model with genuine, portable speedups (0.5–2% accuracy cost) — DistilBERT-style layer dropping is the aggressive end.
- **Gradual magnitude pruning** (cubic schedule during training) beats one-shot pruning; **sparsity + quantization** combine to ~8× on Ampere.
