## Distillation vs Pruning

Pruning starts from a big model and removes parts. **Knowledge distillation** (Hinton et al., 2015) goes the other way: it trains a *small model from scratch*, but instead of learning from raw labels alone, the student learns from the rich output of a large, already-trained **teacher**. Done well it's the most powerful compression technique — but unlike PTQ it requires a full training pipeline.

---

## Dark Knowledge: Why Soft Labels Teach More

A trained network's full output distribution (before `argmax`) carries far more information than the one-hot label it was trained on:

<DarkKnowledge caption="The hard label says only 'dog.' The teacher's soft probabilities say 'dog — and it looks a bit like a husky and a fox, not at all like a cat.' Those inter-class similarities are 'dark knowledge': the teacher's entire learned structure of how classes relate, which a one-hot label throws away." />

Training the student on these soft targets transfers not just the decision boundary but the teacher's whole sense of class geometry. That extra signal acts as a powerful regulariser and lets a small student reach accuracy it could never find from hard labels alone.

---

## The Distillation Loss

The student is trained on a weighted sum of two losses: a **soft** loss matching the teacher, and a **hard** loss matching the ground truth. **Temperature** `T` controls how much the teacher's distribution is softened — higher `T` spreads the probability mass and exposes more of the inter-class structure:

<Formula label="Combined distillation loss" caption="KL pulls the student toward the teacher's softened distribution; CE keeps it anchored to the true label. α≈0.9, T≈4 are typical.">L = α·T²·KL(student_T ‖ teacher_T) + (1−α)·CE(student, labels)</Formula>

<DistillationLoss caption="Both teacher and student logits are softened by T before the KL term; the student is also scored against hard labels by cross-entropy. The two are blended by α — usually 0.9, mostly distillation." />

<Callout type="pitfall" title="Don't forget the T² factor">

Softening logits by `T` shrinks their gradients by roughly `T²`. Without multiplying the KD term back by `T²`, the distillation signal becomes negligibly small next to the cross-entropy gradient, and the student basically ignores the teacher. The `T²` correction restores the balance — it's the single most common distillation bug.

</Callout>

## Feature Distillation: Beyond the Output

Matching only final predictions is the simplest form. **Feature distillation** additionally aligns the teacher's *intermediate* representations, giving the student "hints" about how to process information, not just what to conclude:

<FeatureDistillation caption="An MSE loss matches the student's intermediate feature maps to the teacher's. Because the student is narrower, a small 1×1-conv adapter projects its features up to the teacher's dimensionality first. FitNets pioneered this; PKD applies it layer-by-layer for BERT." />

<Callout type="why" title="Self-distillation and data-free variants">

Distillation has surprising relatives. **Self-distillation** uses the same model as teacher and student — *Born-Again Networks* train generation G1 from G0, G2 from G1, and each generation **improves**, even with no compression (a still-not-fully-explained regularisation effect). **Data-free distillation** handles the case where the teacher's training data is unavailable (privacy/licensing) by using the teacher itself to synthesise samples that maximally activate each class — used in federated and privacy-preserving ML.

</Callout>

## DistilBERT: the Benchmark Result

The canonical success story: **DistilBERT** (Sanh et al., 2019) distilled BERT-base (110M params, 12 layers) into a 6-layer, 66M-parameter student by halving the layers and training on three objectives at once — masked-language-modelling, distillation loss on the MLM outputs, and a cosine loss on hidden states (feature distillation):

<Callout type="key" title="When to reach for distillation">

Distillation is the **largest-gain** compression tool but the **most expensive** to run — it needs a teacher, a training loop, and tuning. Reach for it when quantization and pruning haven't hit your size/latency target and you have a training pipeline available. It also composes: a distilled student can then be quantized and pruned, stacking all three of this module's techniques.

</Callout>

## Recap

- **Distillation trains a small student to mimic a large teacher** — the most powerful compression method, at the cost of a training pipeline.
- The teacher's **soft labels** carry **dark knowledge** (inter-class similarity) that one-hot labels lack, acting as a rich regulariser for the student.
- The loss blends a **temperature-softened KL** term with hard-label **cross-entropy** (`α≈0.9`, `T≈4`); the **T² factor** is essential or the soft signal vanishes.
- **Feature distillation** additionally matches intermediate representations (via a 1×1 adapter), transferring *how* the teacher thinks, not just its output.
- **DistilBERT's 40/60/97** (smaller/faster/accuracy retained) is the target; distillation stacks with quantization and pruning.
