## From Techniques to Process

You now have the full toolkit — quantization, pruning, distillation, graph optimization, hardware tuning. The difference between a junior who *knows* these and a senior who *ships* with them is **process**: applying them in the right order, measuring at every step, and stopping when the target is met. This capstone is that process.

---

## The Five-Phase Decision Flow

Apply techniques in order of **payoff-per-risk** — cheap, zero-risk wins first; accuracy-risky changes only if you still need more:

<OptimizationPipeline caption="Phase 1 characterises (profile, find whether you're compute- or memory-bound from the roofline). Phase 2 takes the free wins (BF16, Flash Attention, torch.compile). Phase 3 quantizes (moderate risk). Phase 4 changes the architecture (pruning, distillation — higher risk). Phase 5 does hardware-specific compilation (zero risk again). Benchmark and verify accuracy between every phase." />

<Callout type="key" title="The order is the lesson">

Most teams jump straight to phase 4 (pruning/distillation) and wonder why they got 2× when they needed 10× — they skipped the cheaper, safer 4× sitting in phases 2–3. **BF16 + Flash + torch.compile + INT8** alone often delivers most of the win with almost no accuracy cost and no training. Exhaust the low-risk phases before you spend training time or accuracy budget.

</Callout>

## A Worked Journey: ResNet-50

Theory becomes concrete in the cumulative log. Starting from the Lab 5-A FP32 baseline, each technique stacks on the last:

<ResNet50Journey caption="42.3 ms → 3.2 ms — a 13.2× speedup for a 0.9% top-1 drop, achieved by stacking BF16, torch.compile, INT8 PTQ, TensorRT, and a final 30% channel-prune + QAT. Memory fell from 98 MB to 18 MB. Crucially, every step is measured and the accuracy impact documented." />

<Callout type="insight" title="Read the curve, not just the endpoint">

Notice the shape: the **early, zero-risk** steps (BF16, compile) already bought 2.6× for ~0.1% accuracy. INT8 + TensorRT pushed to 8.3× for ~0.5%. Only the final pruning step spent real accuracy (0.9% total) for the last 1.6×. If your target had been "5× faster," you'd have stopped after TensorRT — at **zero meaningful accuracy loss**. The pipeline tells you not just how to optimize, but **when to stop**.

</Callout>

## Module Summary: The Optimization Engineer's Toolkit

- **Quantization** is the first tool — INT8 is nearly free (2–4×, &lt;0.5%); AWQ-INT4 extends to 4–8× for LLMs; QAT is the accuracy insurance policy.
- **Pruning** — structured (channel/head) gives portable speedups; unstructured only helps with NVIDIA 2:4; gradual beats post-hoc.
- **Distillation** — the biggest gains but needs training; DistilBERT's 40/60/97 is the bar; soft labels + feature matching together.
- **Graph optimization** — Flash Attention is non-negotiable for transformers; fusion and `torch.compile()` are zero-risk; TensorRT goes furthest.
- **Hardware-aware** — align tiles, quantize the KV cache, use speculative decoding, and pick efficient architectures; always benchmark on the real target.

---

## Recap

- Optimization is a **process**, not a bag of tricks: **Characterise → Quick wins → Quantization → Architecture → Hardware tuning**, ordered by accuracy risk.
- **Measure every step** with the same protocol (accuracy, latency, VRAM, size) — and know **when to stop** (the target, not the maximum).
- The **ResNet-50 journey** (13.2× for 0.9%) shows the shape: most of the win is in the early zero-risk phases; spend accuracy only when you must.
- Internalise the **thresholds** so you can predict and sanity-check results before benchmarking.
