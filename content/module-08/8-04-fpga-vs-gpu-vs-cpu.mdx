## Three Philosophies

CPU, GPU, and FPGA aren't just faster/slower versions of each other — they embody different design philosophies, and the right choice flows from matching the workload to the philosophy:

<HardwarePhilosophies caption="The CPU is a versatile sequential machine; the GPU is thousands of cores hungry for regular parallel math; the FPGA is blank logic that becomes the exact circuit you need. Each is best at — and worst at — different things." />

---

## Why FPGA Wins at Real-Time: Determinism

For the same INT8 CNN, a CPU takes 15–50 ms, a GPU 2–5 ms, an FPGA 0.1–0.5 ms. But the latency *number* isn't the whole story — the **jitter** is:

<LatencyJitter caption="A CPU's latency varies by 5–20 ms (OS scheduling, paging); a GPU's by 1–3 ms (kernel launch, thermal throttling). An FPGA hardware pipeline takes exactly pipeline_depth × clock_period every single input — sub-microsecond jitter, truly deterministic." />

<Callout type="why" title="For safety-critical systems, jitter matters more than speed">

An autonomous vehicle, industrial controller, or medical device that *usually* responds in 2 ms but *occasionally* takes 20 ms is **less safe** than one that *always* responds in exactly 0.5 ms. The FPGA's determinism comes from physics: a hardware pipeline does the same number of clock cycles for every input regardless of OS state, temperature, other processes, or memory pressure. There's no scheduler, no cache miss, no kernel-launch queue — just a fixed-depth pipeline clocking forward.

</Callout>

## Why FPGA Wins at the Edge: Power

To classify ~1000 images/second, you don't need a datacenter GPU — and the efficiency gap is stark:

<EfficiencyBars caption="Images/sec per watt for INT8 CNN inference. A 5 W Xilinx ZCU104 FPGA delivers ~2000 img/s/W — beating even a 70 W datacenter T4 (1857) and crushing a 15 W Jetson Orin (133). For battery devices this is the difference between 30-day operation and hours." />

## The Decision Matrix

<Callout type="key" title="Choosing the right silicon">

**Use FPGA when:** latency must be deterministic (&lt;1 ms, zero jitter); power budget &lt;10 W with throughput &gt;100 ops/s; the model is **fixed** (resynthesis takes hours); you want **custom data widths** (2/4-bit); you're processing **streaming** sensor data sample-by-sample; or unit cost matters at scale. **Not** if the model changes often or is larger than ~10 MB, or you need FP32 throughout.

**Use GPU when:** the model is large (&gt;10 MB), you're **training**, doing **batch** inference, or the model changes frequently (just reload weights). **Not** under a 5 W budget or when latency must be deterministic.

**Use CPU when:** the model is tiny (&lt;1 MB) at low throughput, you need complex pre/post-processing, or you're developing/testing. **Not** for high throughput or energy efficiency.

</Callout>

This is the same workload-to-hardware reasoning from the roofline model (Module 2), now extended to the full accelerator spectrum — and it's exactly the lens we'll carry into the edge microcontrollers later in this module, which push the FPGA's "deterministic, low-power, fixed-model" niche down to milliwatts.

## Recap

- **CPU / GPU / FPGA** embody three philosophies: versatile-sequential, parallel-throughput, and become-the-circuit.
- **FPGA wins on determinism** — latency = pipeline_depth × clock_period with **sub-µs jitter**, which matters more than raw speed for safety-critical real-time systems.
- **FPGA wins on power** — ~2000 img/s/W beats a datacenter GPU, enabling battery/edge deployment.
- **GPU wins on large models** — the FPGA's 630 KB on-chip memory rules out LLMs and big nets; it owns **tiny, fixed, streaming** models at ultra-low power.
- The **decision matrix**: FPGA for deterministic/low-power/fixed/streaming, GPU for large/training/batch/changing, CPU for tiny/control/dev.
