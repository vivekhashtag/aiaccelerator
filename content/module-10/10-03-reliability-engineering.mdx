## Why AI Reliability Is Different

"Reliability" means something different here than in traditional software:

1. **AI failures are probabilistic.** A sort either works or it doesn't. An LLM response is sometimes excellent, sometimes mediocre, occasionally wrong, rarely harmful. Reliability is about **controlling the distribution of outcomes**, not eliminating failures.
2. **Failures are often silent.** A normal API returns 200 or throws. An LLM returns **200 with a confidently wrong answer** — invisible to standard uptime checks.
3. **Failures compound in agents.** A wrong answer in step 3 of a 10-step agent corrupts every later step, even when steps 4–10 execute without technical errors.

<Callout type="key" title="You cannot monitor AI reliability with uptime checks">

A green status page can sit on top of an agent that's quietly wrong 8% of the time. That's why this lesson — and Module 9's evaluation work — exists: AI reliability must be measured at the level of *output quality and task success*, not just *was the service reachable*.

</Callout>

## The Reliability Stack

Production AI reliability is engineered at four independent levels, each with its own SLA and measurement:

<ReliabilityStack caption="Level 1 Infrastructure (retry/backoff, circuit breaker, provider fallback — 99.9% uptime). Level 2 Response quality (schema validation, LLM-judge, canary — <5% low-quality). Level 3 Agent (task success, budget, escalation — >90% success, <$2/task). Level 4 Safety (in/out classifiers, PII, audit — <0.1% harmful)." />

- **Level 1 — Infrastructure.** Standard concerns: retry with backoff, circuit breakers (Module 9), **multi-provider fallback**, rate-limit queues, health checks. *SLA:* 99.9% uptime (8.7 h/year). *Measure:* error rate, P50/P95/P99 latency.
- **Level 2 — Response quality.** Output validation (Pydantic), LLM-as-judge on production traffic, A/B and canary before rollout, semantic similarity checks. *SLA:* &lt;5% low-quality (judge &lt; 3.5/5). *Measure:* judge scores sampled at ~1% of traffic.
- **Level 3 — Agent.** Task success rate, step efficiency, cost per task, human-escalation paths, replay testing. *SLA:* &gt;90% success, &lt;$2/task. *Measure:* structured logging of goal/success/cost per run.
- **Level 4 — Safety.** Input/output classifiers (Llama Guard), prompt-injection detection, PII redaction, audit logging, red-teaming. *SLA:* &lt;0.1% harmful. *Measure:* automated classifiers + human-review sampling.

Level 1 reliability has one AI-specific addition worth showing — **fall back to another provider** when your primary is down:

```python
def generate(messages, primary="claude-opus-4-8"):
    for model, client in [(primary, anthropic_client), ("gpt-4o", openai_client)]:
        try:
            return with_retry(lambda: client.create(model=model, messages=messages))
        except (RateLimitError, ServiceUnavailable):
            continue        # primary down → try the next provider
    raise RuntimeError("all providers unavailable")
```

**Step by step:**

1. **`with_retry`** (from Module 9.9) handles transient blips on each provider before giving up — the inner reliability layer.
2. **The loop is the outer layer**: if the whole primary provider is rate-limited or down, it falls through to a second provider rather than failing the user. Provider diversity is an availability strategy unique to API-based AI.
3. **Only after all options are exhausted** does it raise — and even then, a production system would degrade gracefully (cached answer, "try later") rather than error.

## The Deployment Pipeline

A prompt or model change is a production change with real cost and quality stakes — so it ships through five gated stages, not a direct push:

<DeploymentPipeline caption="Offline eval (must beat baseline on the test suite) → shadow (real traffic, outputs hidden, compare old vs new) → canary (1–5% of users, watch 24–48h) → progressive rollout (10→25→50→100%, auto-rollback on regression) → full production (keep old version deployable for 7 days)." />

**Step by step through the gates:**

1. **Offline eval** — run the Module 9.9 test suite against the new version; it must match or beat the baseline on every category, and you check the cost impact (did token count change?).
2. **Shadow** — the new version receives *copies* of real traffic, but its outputs are **not** shown to users; you compare old vs new on real inputs with zero user risk.
3. **Canary** — 1–5% of real users get the new version; you watch quality, latency, error rate, and satisfaction signals for 24–48 hours.
4. **Progressive rollout** — 10 → 25 → 50 → 100% over days, with **automated rollback** if any metric degrades.
5. **Full production** — all traffic on the new version, but the old one stays deployable for 7 days for emergency rollback.

<Callout type="why" title="Why so much ceremony for 'just a prompt'?">

Because at scale a prompt change *is* an infrastructure change. A revision that costs 20% more tokens, or is wrong 2% more often, can mean tens of thousands of dollars or serious erosion of user trust — silently, because the service still returns 200s. The pipeline converts a probabilistic, silent-failure-prone change into a measured, reversible one. This is the deployment-side answer to "AI failures are silent."

</Callout>

## Recap

- AI reliability differs from software reliability: failures are **probabilistic, often silent, and compound in agents** — uptime checks miss them entirely.
- Reliability is engineered at **four levels** — infrastructure, response quality, agent, safety — each with its own SLA and measurement; ignoring any one leaves the system exposed.
- **Multi-provider fallback** is an AI-specific availability lever at the infrastructure level (retry → fall back → degrade gracefully).
- A prompt/model change ships through a **five-stage gated pipeline** (offline eval → shadow → canary → progressive → full) with automated rollback — because at scale a prompt change is an infrastructure change with silent failure modes.
